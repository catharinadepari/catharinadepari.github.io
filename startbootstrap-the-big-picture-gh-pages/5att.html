Place Attachment

This page is dedicated for explaining place attachment concepts and how they apply to the context of villagers in Mount Merapi.

What is Place Attachment?

Interest in place attachment grew in 1970s as notable psychologist, John Bowbly, began his study on the effects of maternal deprivation in children. He evidenced that emotional attachment to a parent figure was a part of systems of maintaining a stable relationship with family. In addition, Fried (1963) studied the psychological effects of the forced dislocation on residents living in Boston’s suburb in the course of urban redevelopment. Deep mourning following the relocation indicates the disruptive effects of this policy to the people’s sense of continuity. Sociology contributed to place attachment theories by introducing the concept of community attachment in 1977. Some sociologists theorized that attachment is not a unitary phenomenon but made up of four independent dimensions comprising of institutional ties, social activity, social interactions, and affective attachment which could be measured by two indicators, satisfaction with the neighborhoods and desires for residential stability. In cases where people are dependent upon a place for specific reasons such as personal needs, the social and economic ties could also serve as variables that explain the place attachment (Gerson, Stueve, and Fischer mentioned in Giuliani 2003). The other theoretical contribution to place attachment was made by a human geographer, Tuan, who first introduced the notion of attachment to place through his publication Topophilia in 1974. Human experience and emotional significance are argued by Tuan as what create a place and also distinguishes it from a geographic space. Another implication of this field to place attachment is the emphasis on human experience as essential factor to understand the place attachment (Giuliani, 2003, p.147). In 1981, Shumaker and Taylor formulated a model of attachment that combined the concepts of satisfaction and attachment, and developed theory of place dependence. Attachment in place dependence conception is understood as associations between individuals and their residential environments based on functional value. The intensity of the bond is determined by the extent to which the physical and social characteristics of the environment could meet the inhabitants’ needs and provide possible alternatives and choices. Before them, Proshansky had already introduced another concept in place attachment, that is place identity in 1978. Differ from place dependence, this concept explains attachment based on the specific characteristics of the place, the quality of the social features associated with the place, and individual’s capacity to adapt to or to transform the place (Giuliani 2003, 151). Altman in 1975 emphasized the effects of territoriality on place attachment. Human territoriality he argued, defines the strong relationship that individuals have with particular setting. Feelings of possessiveness and attempts of controlling the space characterize the territoriality. Territory is highly associated with self-image or identity (Brown, 1987 mentioned in Giuliani, 2003, p.154) which corresponds with self-consciousness. Because of this, place identity became an interest of many phenomenological studies. Phenomenology contributed to place attachment and place identity theories by providing methods of revealing the people’s subjective experience in particular environment (Lalli 1992, 287).

How does it apply to Mount Merapi?

During my field-survey back in 2019, I found some interesting themes emerged from my interviews with villagers living in Palemsari Village, Umbulharjo, Cangkringan Disctrict. Despite the eruption devasting impacts in 2010, the villagers returned to their former high risk areas and others established new settlement which is located relatively nearby their former homes located about 5 kms around the volcano's crater. However, most of them routinely occupied their former lands to perform the geo-tourism business, worked either as parking keeper, tourist's guides, food vendors, or jeep drivers. The villagers perform this activity collectively and share the daily incomes for the whole households.

Women who continued the dairy farming, struggle every morning to seek wildgrass in the deep forest regardless the risks they encounter while doing this.This partly shows their high dependence on the natural resources of the volcano which defines the people's attachment to their places. However, emotional based factors such as childhood memories might be more dominant than the economic or functional reasons in explaining this phenomenon.

We hear the eruption when we collect grass in the forest..We also see falling rocks, erupted by the volcano..On Tuesday…yes, yesterday..going further to the north, we tried to collect grass…It is 4,5 kilometers from our former kampoeng..To find them, we should reach 3 kilometer from the crater…We have to be hurry when collecting the grass..We always observe the volcano..If it releases lava, we will run and leave everything behind..If it just releases rocks, alhamdullilah, it is fine..Each farmer has his or her own friends when going to the forest. We go to upper areas at 7 a.m. every morning and reaches the areas at 8 a.m.
I am with my wife..Sometimes we go at 6.30 a.m…We leave our motorcycle at the former kampoeng of Kinahrejo and continue our journey by walking. It takes 2 hours to collect the grass..During dry season, we must go further to the forest which is 2 kilometers away from the crater. The grass are not abundant but we still can find some..In the forest, the grass are depleted since they are competed with large trees..For that 2 kilometer of distance, I only walk”
From Interview with local villagers in Palemsari in 2019
